Step 1: Choosing What Keyboard You Want

The hardest step in the process is figuring out what you want from your first custom mechanical keyboard.

To simply things, here is what you will need to figure out.

  • What size keyboard you need
  • What features you prefer
  • When you want to build your keyboard

If you want to choose exactly what features, size, and when you want to build your keyboard, we'll go over each aspect below.

Finding the Right Size Keyboard

The great thing about custom mechanical keyboards is that there are a ton of different keyboard sizes that can be built.

This may make it incredibly difficult to choose the correct size as you need to understand what all different sizes include and if you are comfortable with them or not.

For your convenience, here is a quick list of all different keyboard sizes:

  • Full-sized: You usually can’t find this size for custom keyboards, it’s only used on pre-built keyboards.
  • 1800-Compact (96%): A super cool layout size that axes the navigational cluster and smushes the number pad with the rest of the keys.
  • Tenkeyless: A common layout that chops off the number pad but still keeps the rest of the keyboard layout.
  • 75%: Similar to tenkeyless, except the navigational cluster is placed vertically to save space. Fun and comfortable to use.
  • 65%: This layout removes the function row and keeps some of the navigational cluster. It’s also the smallest keyboard size that keeps the dedicated arrow keys.
  • 60%: The most common custom keyboard layout. There is no function row, arrow keys, or navigational cluster.
  • 40%: The smallest size that you can still type on. It’s basically a 60% keyboard except you remove the numeric keys. You’ll need to get used to typing on different layers to use this size.
  • Macropad: Basically, a separate number pad that you can assign macros to. Very fun to use alongside your main keyboard.

Picking a size for your keyboard is the first crucial step.

Uncommon sizes like Macropad or 40% may take longer for parts to arrive, but parts for more common layouts may arrive within a short period of time.

Picking Out The Features

Here are a few features people may want to look for in a keyboard:

  • RGB lighting: Essentially a setting you can tweak to change the brightness of your keyboard setup.
  • QMK Firmware: This changes the ability for you to program your keyboard to remap keys if necessary.
  • USB Port: USB-C is typically recommended since it is reversible, and you won't need to worry about plugging it in upside-down. Location of the port also matters depending on preferences.

There are other parts to be dealt with as well, but those will gone over in the next step where we pick out the parts.

The Timeline for Building Your Keyboard

There are two ways to get the parts for your keyboard. Either by individual in-stock parts or by group buys.

  1. In-Stock Parts: This is the faster way to get parts. Going to the online store and ordering the part allows them to be shipped out within a couple of weeks or days.
  2. Group Buys: If you want really customized parts or super unique builds, you'll probably go through a group buy, which may take a much longer time. (possibly up to 6-9 months)

Ultimately, it is up to you to decide if you want the keyboard built now or months later depending on how customized you want your keyboard to be.
